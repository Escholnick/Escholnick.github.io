Cosmos Week 1

Design Challenge

The core of this weeks design challenge was to build a robot which was mobile and could be used to pop balloons placed on the back of other robots, while avoiding having its own balloon popped. It was sort of like a classic childrens game of "flag tag", though modified to be simpler for robots than the complex task of removing an object from a stable location. I mentally broke the challenge down into several component parts:

1. Construction

The goal of this task was to construct the chassis of the robot,and attach the basic drive motors and wheels, using a premade kit with laser-cut parts and screws.

2. Mobility

The goal of this task was to properly wire the motors to be used in driving, and to write the code that would allow the robot to move as commanded

3. Offense

The goal of this task was to create one or more devices which could be used to pop the balloons of other robots, and possibly program them to move if needed

Daily Work

Day 1

On day 1, we began assembly of the robot chassis, and I learned the basics of state machines and plotted out the overall code design for the robot. This involved deciding what states the robot would have (I went with 5, for driving forward, reversing, each turning direction, and stopped). We got the basics set up when it came to the Raspberry PI we were going to be using as a brain for the robot.

Day 2

Day 2 was much more fruitful. We began by finishing the assembly of the robot chassis, and then moved on to setting up the drive motors. The first draft of the wiring was done by me, but it was subsequently cleaned up by my teammate Arhan. We then also worked on connecting our controller to the PI, and then decyphering the control scheme and how to read input.

Once we got the motors properly setup and had figured out the input schema, I began work on our controller script. I designed it around a finite state machine with five states. The "d-pad" is the primary method of control, where each of the 4 "direction arrows" relates to one of the non-rest states. Depressing that key transitions to that state (with exceptions for counter states like trying to turn in opposite directions simultaneously) and releasing that key returns the robot to its rest state.

Day 3

Day 3 was probably the most impactful when it came to building the robot. I finished the mobility script, and we were able to successfully test drive the robot. The majority of the day, however, was spent on weapons. We had initially had the idea of building a defensive pivot which would lift the balloon out of reach of all the other robots once engaged, however we were eventually told that the location where the balloon would be placed on the robot would prevent that idea. This led us to creating two weapons. One which would pop a balloon from above, by swinging downwards, and one which would oscilate side-to-side to pop balloons. The work on these weapons took the remainder of the day, and the oscilating weapon was not entirely finished.

Day 4

Day 4 was the final day of work before the actual competition, though it was relatively unimpactful for our group. All we did was finish the oscillating weapon and take photos to document what we had done. We tested both weapons and found the oscilator to be more effective, though both were able to pop the balloons we tested them on.

Day 5

Day 5 was primarily a competition day. There were two main competitions, each with two rounds, as well as a final non-competitive event.
The first competition was a free-for-all format. Each robot had a balloon taped to its back, and then on a signal, everyone worked to pop the other group's balloons without having their own popped. There were two rounds, one with each member of the pair as a driver. We were 5th of 11 in the first round, and 8th in the second, though we were able to pop the previous round's winner before getting out.
The second competion was a team based battle. We were drafted by the winners of the two free-for-all matches, and made to fight it out against the other team. The first round was just an all-out fight, but the second introduced team "flags" which the other team needed to pop to win. Our team won the first round, and got our flag captured during the second one, though we still managed to eliminate all the robots on the other team.

Technical Breakdown

For those who are interested, here is a more technical breakdown of how the robot works

Hardware

The basic robot was a two-wheeled differential drive car, controlled by a bluetooth USB gamepad modeled after an SNES controller.
The core of the robot was a Raspberry PI four as the brain with an off-the-shelf motor controller and PWM board, all wired together using a breadboard.
Driving was done with uncontrolled motors driven through direct PWM signals from the PI, while the weapons were built using positional servos run through a PWM board.
The PI is powered by a USB-C battery pack, and the motors are powered by a 6v pack made up of 4 1.5v rechargeable AA batteries.

Software

The software was written in python and used a finite state machine architecture. My group made this design decision based on the fact that a lecture just prior to the project was on finite state machines and how to use them in robotics. It was decided that an FSM was probably the desired architecture based on the lecture, and so that's what we went with. The machine actually had three state machines running in parallel.

The first was the "drive machine" and had five states: Stopped, Moving Forward, Reversing, Turning Left, and Turning Right. Certain inputs from the USB gamepad caused the machine to activate or deactivate the drive motors and change states.

The other two were the "weapon machines" and each had two states. The hammer weapon/pike had an "up" state and a "down" state, while the oscilator weapon had a "left" state and a "right" state. Gamepad inputs triggered both a state change and a swing of the weapon.
